# Phonetics Introduction: Translating Spelling to Sound

One of the most powerful applications of Finite State Transducers (FSTs) is the automated translation of written text into its phonetic representation, usually via the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA). For a language like Irish, this technology is not merely a technical curiosity—it is a vital tool for learners struggling to bridge the gap between complex orthography and actual pronunciation.

## The Systematic Nature of Irish Spelling

To an outsider, Irish spelling can appear dauntingly opaque. However, beneath the clusters of vowels and unfamiliar consonant combinations lies a highly disciplined and rule-based system. Irish orthography is designed to signal the "quality" of consonants—whether they are "broad" (velarized) or "slender" (palatalized).

Because this system is so consistent, it is ideally suited for a transducer model. An FST can be programmed with the phonological rules of the language to perform a deterministic rewrite. While the underlying morphological changes (like a word changing shape during declension) are a separate, more complex modeling task, the act of converting a fixed string of letters into a string of sounds is a straightforward and rewarding implementation.

## Value for the Learner

For students of Irish, an FST-based pronunciation guide provides immediate clarity in several key areas:

**The Broad and Slender System:** In Irish, many vowels are written not to be pronounced as distinct sounds, but purely to indicate the "broad" or "slender" status of the adjacent consonants. For example, in a word like <span class="ga-word">Siobhán<span class="ga-tooltip">/ʃʊwəːnˠ/</span></span>, the 'i' and 'o' serve to "slenderize" the 'S' and "broaden" the 'bh', respectively. An FST can strip away these technical spelling markers and output the unambiguous IPA phoneme `/ʃ/`, showing the student exactly what to ignore and what to emphasize.

**Initial Mutations (Eclipsis):** When a word undergoes eclipsis, a new consonant is placed directly in front of the original starting letter. In written Irish, both letters are kept: <span class="ga-word">bád<span class="ga-tooltip">/bˠaːd̪ˠ/</span></span> becomes <span class="ga-word">mbád<span class="ga-tooltip">/mˠaːd̪ˠ/</span></span>. A learner might be tempted to try and pronounce both the 'm' and the 'b', which is incorrect. An FST model perfectly captures this by "eclipsing" the original sound on the output tape. It reads "mb" and writes only the nasal `/m/`, providing an absolute source of truth for the student that the 'b' is silent.

## A Rule-Based Approach

Implementing a phonetic transducer is a fairly accessible project because Irish phonetics are largely deterministic. Unlike English, which has many historical spelling inconsistencies, Irish follows its rulebook with high fidelity. By chaining together simple "rewrite" rules (e.g., "if you see vowels `a, o, u` around this consonant, output a broad version"), a developer can build a robust engine that accurately generates pronunciation for any Irish word, including those the system has never encountered before.

By focusing purely on the pronunciation of strings, this component of the Aithesc.io environment provides the foundational data needed for digital dictionaries, audio glossaries, and advanced text-to-speech systems, making the rhythmic beauty of the Irish language accessible to everyone.
