# Irish Phonotactics and Connected Speech

The study of Irish morphology often focuses on the "canonical" form of words—how a lemma is inflected for case, gender, or tense in isolation. However, in natural, spoken Irish, words are rarely pronounced as isolated lexical units. Instead, they interact with their neighbors in a complex system of **Phonotactics** and **Connected Speech** rules.

This reality creates a classic conflict in computational linguistics: **Lexical Phonology** (the rules governing how a single word is formed) vs. **Post-Lexical/Prosodic Phonology** (how those words are merged in a sentence). While our FST engine Currently generates phonetically accurate word-level IPA, a natural "sentence level" representation requires understanding how words influence each other across boundaries.

## 1. Proclitics and Vowel Reduction

Many functional words in Irish—such as articles (*an, na*), particles (*ag, ní, do*), and prepositions—are **proclitics**. They carry no lexical stress and "lean" on the following stressed word (usually the noun or verb). 

Because they are unstressed, these words undergo **vowel reduction** to a neutral schwa `/ə/`. A word-level model might preserve the full vowel quality (e.g., article <span class="ga-word">na<span class="ga-tooltip">/nˠa/</span></span> as `/nˠa/`), but in natural speech, it is almost always `/nə/`. In a long sentence, the rhythmic prosody of Irish makes it impossible for every word to carry primary stress.

## 2. The "Sandhi" Effect: Consonant Elision

One of the most frequent phonotactic rules in Irish involves the prepositional particle <span class="ga-word">ag<span class="ga-tooltip">/aɡ/</span></span> (used for the progressive aspect). This is a prime example of a **Sandhi** effect—where sounds change at word boundaries.

*   **Canonical Look-up:** Treates <span class="ga-word">ag<span class="ga-tooltip">/aɡ/</span></span> as an isolated lexical unit, retaining the voiced velar plosive `/ɡ/`.
*   **Connected Speech:** Recognizes that the final consonant of <span class="ga-word">ag<span class="ga-tooltip">/aɡ/</span></span> is elided before another consonant to ensure phonetic flow.
    *   *Rule:* <span class="ga-word">ag<span class="ga-tooltip">/aɡ/</span></span> + [Consonant] → `/ə/` (e.g., <span class="ga-word">ag rith<span class="ga-tooltip">/aɡ ɾʲɪh/</span></span> → `/ə ɾˠɪ/`)
    *   *Rule:* <span class="ga-word">ag<span class="ga-tooltip">/aɡ/</span></span> + [Vowel] → `/əɡ/` (e.g., <span class="ga-word">ag ól<span class="ga-tooltip">/aɡ ɔːlˠ/</span></span> → `/əɡ oːlˠ/`)

## 3. Phonological Influence Across Word Boundaries

Irish exhibits firm rules for how certain words influence the pronunciation of the starting phonemes of following words. Beyond the initial consonant mutations (like Lenition and Eclipsis) which are triggered by grammatical syntax, there are more "fluid" phonetic interactions:

*   **Linking /h/:** Prevents vowel hiatus (clashing) between two words (e.g., <span class="ga-word">le ól<span class="ga-tooltip">/lʲɛ ɔːlˠ/</span></span> → <span class="ga-word">le hól<span class="ga-tooltip">/lʲɛ hˠɔːlˠ/</span></span>).
*   **T-prothesis:** Acts as a phonetic buffer in specific articled contexts.
*   **Consonant Cluster Simplification:** When two words meet with similar consonants at the boundary, they often merge or simplify to maintain the language's rhythmic melody.

## 4. Modeling Strategy: The Sandhi Level

For a computational model, the goal is not just to be "dictionary accurate" but to be "phonologically realistic." For tasks like TTS (Text-to-Speech) or advanced linguistic modeling, we can add a **Sandhi Level** transducer to our FST pipeline. 

This level sits after the lexical and morphological tapes, applying a "Phonological Phrase" tape that implements contraction and elision rules (e.g., `g -> 0 / # _ [+Consonant]`). This transforms the "robotic" word-by-word output into the natural, fluid prosody of a native speaker.
