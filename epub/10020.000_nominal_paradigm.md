# Nominal Paradigm

In the context of the Irish Morphological Parser, the **Nominal Paradigm**
handles the inflectional rules for nouns and adjectives. To understand how we
model this in an FST, we must first define what a "paradigm" is and how it
manifests in the Irish language.

## What is a Paradigm?

In linguistics, a **paradigm** is a complete set of related word forms that
share the same root but differ in their grammatical features (such as case,
number, or gender). Think of it as a "template" or "matrix" of all possible
variations a single word can take.

For a noun, the paradigm consists of its forms in the:

- **Nominative Case** (Subject/Direct Object)
- **Genitive Case** (Possessive)
- **Vocative Case** (Direct Address)
- **Dative Case** (Following certain prepositions—though in Modern Irish, this
  has largely merged with the nominative except in specific dialects or fixed
  phrases).

### Applying the Paradigm: The 1st Declension

Take the 1st declension noun **<ga ipa="/kat̪ˠ/">cat</ga>** (cat). Its paradigm defines a specific
set of behavior that is shared by **every single noun in this class**:

- **Nom. Sing.:** <ga ipa="/kat̪ˠ/">cat</ga>
- **Gen. Sing.:** <ga ipa="/katʲ/">cait</ga> (Slenderization/Palatalization of the final consonant)
- **Nom. Plur.:** <ga ipa="/katʲ/">cait</ga> (In this declension, Nom. Plural often matches Gen.
  Singular)
- **Gen. Plur.:** <ga ipa="/kat̪ˠ/">cat</ga> (Zero-ending)

The Morphotactic FST does not treat <ga ipa="/kat̪ˠ/">cat</ga> as a unique exception; instead, it
uses the **declension class tag** provided by the Lexicon (e.g., `<D1>`) as a
primary logic switch. When the engine sees `cat<N><MASC><D1><GEN><SG>`, it
ignores the fact that the word is "cat" and simply follows the mass rules for
**Declension 1**. This same logic applies to <ga ipa="/bˠaːd̪ˠ/">bád</ga> (boat) → <ga ipa="/bˠaːɪdʲ/">báid</ga>, <ga ipa="/kapˠəl̪ˠ/">capall</ga>
(horse) → <ga ipa="/kapˠəl̠ʲ/">capaill</ga>, and thousands of other nouns.

## The Five Noun Declensions

The Irish language organizes its nouns into five distinct declensions. What
makes this system computationally powerful is that each declension acts as a
**distinct set of systematic rules** that apply to all its members. Once we have
modeled the transition rules for a declension, any noun assigned to that class
in the Lexicon will automatically inflect correctly.

**The First Declension** consists almost entirely of **masculine** nouns ending
in a broad consonant. The defining characteristic of this declension is the
"slenderization" (<ga ipa="/kɪːlˠʊː/">caolú</ga>) of the final consonant to form the genitive singular
(e.g., <ga ipa="/bˠaːd̪ˠ/">bád</ga> becomes <ga ipa="/bˠaːɪdʲ/">báid</ga>). The nominative plural is often formed in the same
way, while the genitive plural typically returns to the broad, non-suffixed
stem.

**The Second Declension** is primarily composed of **feminine** nouns ending in
either a broad or slender consonant. To form the genitive singular, a slender
'e' is added to the end of the stem; if the stem ends in a broad consonant, it
must first be slenderized to satisfy the <ga ipa="/kɪːlˠ lʲɛ kɪːlˠ/">Caol le Caol</ga> rule (e.g., <ga ipa="/klˠɔx/">cloch</ga>
becomes <ga ipa="/klˠɔɪçə/">cloiche</ga>). Plural forms in this declension typically involve adding
<ga ipa="/-a/">-a</ga> or <ga ipa="/-axə/">-acha</ga>.

**The Third Declension** contains both **masculine and feminine** nouns. Its
hallmark is the addition of a broad 'a' to form the genitive singular (e.g.,
<ga ipa="/bˠaːd̪ˠɔːɪɾʲ/">bádóir</ga> becomes <ga ipa="/bˠaːd̪ˠɔːɾˠə/">bádóra</ga>). This declension is very stable and includes many
modern occupation terms ending in <ga ipa="/-ɔːɪɾʲ/">-óir</ga> or <ga ipa="/-ʊːɪɾʲ/">-úir</ga>. The plural forms usually
involve appending <ga ipa="/-ɪː/">-í</ga> or other broad suffixes.

**The Fourth Declension** includes **masculine and feminine** nouns, many of
which end in a vowel or the suffix <ga ipa="/-ɪːnʲ/">-ín</ga>. This declension is unique because the
genitive singular is identical to the nominative singular (e.g., <ga ipa="/kalʲəːnʲ/">cailín</ga>
remains <ga ipa="/kalʲəːnʲ/">cailín</ga>). There is no internal vowel shift or suffix added in the
singular, making the "paradigm" for the singular cases a simple mapping of
identity. Plurals are typically formed by adding <ga ipa="/-ɪː/">-í</ga> or <ga ipa="/-ɪjə/">-idhe</ga>.

**The Fifth Declension** is characterized by the addition of a consonant (often
n, d, or th) to form the genitive singular. These are often **feminine**
nouns where the stem itself expands significantly (e.g., <ga ipa="/kahəɾʲ/">cathair</ga> becomes
<ga ipa="/kahɾˠəx/">cathrach</ga>, or <ga ipa="/pʲaɾʲʃə/">pearsa</ga> becomes <ga ipa="/pʲaɾʲʃənˠ/">pearsan</ga>). Because of this expansion, the 5th
declension presents the most complex structural shifts for the Morphotactic FST
to resolve before passing the string to the Morphophonology layer.

## Adjective Agreement

The Nominal Paradigm also governs how adjectives must "agree" with the nouns
they modify. If a noun is feminine and in the nominative singular, the
Morphotactic layer ensures that the following adjective receives a lenition
instruction (`^LEN`). Similarly, if the noun is in the genitive plural, the
adjective must often take specific plural endings. This ensures that the entire
noun-phrase functions as a grammatically cohesive unit.
