# Nominal Paradigm

In the context of the Irish Morphological Parser, the **Nominal Paradigm**
handles the inflectional rules for nouns and adjectives. To understand how we
model this in an FST, we must first define what a "paradigm" is and how it
manifests in the Irish language.

## What is a Paradigm?

In linguistics, a **paradigm** is a complete set of related word forms that
share the same root but differ in their grammatical features (such as case,
number, or gender). Think of it as a "template" or "matrix" of all possible
variations a single word can take.

For a noun, the paradigm consists of its forms in the:

- **Nominative Case** (Subject/Direct Object)
- **Genitive Case** (Possessive)
- **Vocative Case** (Direct Address)
- **Dative Case** (Following certain prepositions—though in Modern Irish, this
  has largely merged with the nominative except in specific dialects or fixed
  phrases).

### Applying the Paradigm: The 1st Declension

Take the 1st declension noun **<span class="ga-word">cat<span class="ga-tooltip">/kat̪ˠ/</span></span>** (cat). Its paradigm defines a specific
set of behavior that is shared by **every single noun in this class**:

- **Nom. Sing.:** <span class="ga-word">cat<span class="ga-tooltip">/kat̪ˠ/</span></span>
- **Gen. Sing.:** <span class="ga-word">cait<span class="ga-tooltip">/katʲ/</span></span> (Slenderization/Palatalization of the final consonant)
- **Nom. Plur.:** <span class="ga-word">cait<span class="ga-tooltip">/katʲ/</span></span> (In this declension, Nom. Plural often matches Gen.
  Singular)
- **Gen. Plur.:** <span class="ga-word">cat<span class="ga-tooltip">/kat̪ˠ/</span></span> (Zero-ending)

The Morphotactic FST does not treat <span class="ga-word">cat<span class="ga-tooltip">/kat̪ˠ/</span></span> as a unique exception; instead, it
uses the **declension class tag** provided by the Lexicon (e.g., `<D1>`) as a
primary logic switch. When the engine sees `cat<N><MASC><D1><GEN><SG>`, it
ignores the fact that the word is "cat" and simply follows the mass rules for
**Declension 1**. This same logic applies to <span class="ga-word">bád<span class="ga-tooltip">/bˠaːd̪ˠ/</span></span> (boat) → <span class="ga-word">báid<span class="ga-tooltip">/bˠaːɪdʲ/</span></span>, <span class="ga-word">capall<span class="ga-tooltip">/kapˠəl̪ˠ/</span></span>
(horse) → <span class="ga-word">capaill<span class="ga-tooltip">/kapˠəl̠ʲ/</span></span>, and thousands of other nouns.

## The Five Noun Declensions

The Irish language organizes its nouns into five distinct declensions. What
makes this system computationally powerful is that each declension acts as a
**distinct set of systematic rules** that apply to all its members. Once we have
modeled the transition rules for a declension, any noun assigned to that class
in the Lexicon will automatically inflect correctly.

**The First Declension** consists almost entirely of **masculine** nouns ending
in a broad consonant. The defining characteristic of this declension is the
"slenderization" (<span class="ga-word">caolú<span class="ga-tooltip">/kɪːlˠʊː/</span></span>) of the final consonant to form the genitive singular
(e.g., <span class="ga-word">bád<span class="ga-tooltip">/bˠaːd̪ˠ/</span></span> becomes <span class="ga-word">báid<span class="ga-tooltip">/bˠaːɪdʲ/</span></span>). The nominative plural is often formed in the same
way, while the genitive plural typically returns to the broad, non-suffixed
stem.

**The Second Declension** is primarily composed of **feminine** nouns ending in
either a broad or slender consonant. To form the genitive singular, a slender
'e' is added to the end of the stem; if the stem ends in a broad consonant, it
must first be slenderized to satisfy the <span class="ga-word">Caol le Caol<span class="ga-tooltip">/kɪːlˠ lʲɛ kɪːlˠ/</span></span> rule (e.g., <span class="ga-word">cloch<span class="ga-tooltip">/klˠɔx/</span></span>
becomes <span class="ga-word">cloiche<span class="ga-tooltip">/klˠɔɪçə/</span></span>). Plural forms in this declension typically involve adding
<span class="ga-word">-a<span class="ga-tooltip">/-a/</span></span> or <span class="ga-word">-acha<span class="ga-tooltip">/-axə/</span></span>.

**The Third Declension** contains both **masculine and feminine** nouns. Its
hallmark is the addition of a broad 'a' to form the genitive singular (e.g.,
<span class="ga-word">bádóir<span class="ga-tooltip">/bˠaːd̪ˠɔːɪɾʲ/</span></span> becomes <span class="ga-word">bádóra<span class="ga-tooltip">/bˠaːd̪ˠɔːɾˠə/</span></span>). This declension is very stable and includes many
modern occupation terms ending in <span class="ga-word">-óir<span class="ga-tooltip">/-ɔːɪɾʲ/</span></span> or <span class="ga-word">-úir<span class="ga-tooltip">/-ʊːɪɾʲ/</span></span>. The plural forms usually
involve appending <span class="ga-word">-í<span class="ga-tooltip">/-ɪː/</span></span> or other broad suffixes.

**The Fourth Declension** includes **masculine and feminine** nouns, many of
which end in a vowel or the suffix <span class="ga-word">-ín<span class="ga-tooltip">/-ɪːnʲ/</span></span>. This declension is unique because the
genitive singular is identical to the nominative singular (e.g., <span class="ga-word">cailín<span class="ga-tooltip">/kalʲəːnʲ/</span></span>
remains <span class="ga-word">cailín<span class="ga-tooltip">/kalʲəːnʲ/</span></span>). There is no internal vowel shift or suffix added in the
singular, making the "paradigm" for the singular cases a simple mapping of
identity. Plurals are typically formed by adding <span class="ga-word">-í<span class="ga-tooltip">/-ɪː/</span></span> or <span class="ga-word">-idhe<span class="ga-tooltip">/-ɪjə/</span></span>.

**The Fifth Declension** is characterized by the addition of a consonant (often
n, d, or th) to form the genitive singular. These are often **feminine**
nouns where the stem itself expands significantly (e.g., <span class="ga-word">cathair<span class="ga-tooltip">/kahəɾʲ/</span></span> becomes
<span class="ga-word">cathrach<span class="ga-tooltip">/kahɾˠəx/</span></span>, or <span class="ga-word">pearsa<span class="ga-tooltip">/pʲaɾʲʃə/</span></span> becomes <span class="ga-word">pearsan<span class="ga-tooltip">/pʲaɾʲʃənˠ/</span></span>). Because of this expansion, the 5th
declension presents the most complex structural shifts for the Morphotactic FST
to resolve before passing the string to the Morphophonology layer.

## Adjective Agreement

The Nominal Paradigm also governs how adjectives must "agree" with the nouns
they modify. If a noun is feminine and in the nominative singular, the
Morphotactic layer ensures that the following adjective receives a lenition
instruction (`^LEN`). Similarly, if the noun is in the genitive plural, the
adjective must often take specific plural endings. This ensures that the entire
noun-phrase functions as a grammatically cohesive unit.
