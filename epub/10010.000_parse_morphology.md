# Parsing Morphology: A Layman's Introduction

When we learn a language, we naturally begin to understand how words are built.
For instance, in English, we know that adding "-s" to "cat" makes it plural
("cats"), or adding "-ed" to "walk" makes it past tense ("walked").

**Morphological parsing** is the process of teaching a computer to do exactly
this: to look at a word and break it down into its core grammatical pieces.

If we give a morphological parser the word **"walked"**, it will analyze it and
return:

- Root: **walk**
- Features: **Verb, Past Tense**

While this seems simple for English, languages like Irish have incredibly
complex rules where the _beginning_ of a word might change based on its context,
or the vowels within the word might shift entirely.

## Why not just use AI and Large Language Models (LLMs)?

With the rise of powerful AI systems like ChatGPT, you might wonder why we need
to manually build parsing systems at all. Can't the AI just learn the language?

The answer lies in how these systems learn. LLMs learn patterns by reading
billions of pages of text. For languages like English, there is more than enough
text for the AI to "guess" the rules of the language with high accuracy.

However, for **under-resourced languages** like Irish or many indigenous
languages, there simply isn't enough high-quality digital text available to
train these massive models perfectly. As a result, LLMs often guess wrong when
speaking Irish. They might invent words that sound Irish but don't exist
(hallucinations), or they might make basic grammatical errors because they don't
actually **understand** the rules—they are just predicting the next most likely
word pattern.

## The Solution: Finite State Transducers (FSTs)

To solve this problem, we use a technology called a **Finite State Transducer
(FST)**.

If an LLM is like an artist who sketches a landscape from memory, an FST is like
a blueprint drawn by an architect.

An FST is a computationally efficient, mathematical model that applies strict,
explicit rules to translate one string of text into another. It works
brilliantly for morphology because it is:

1.  **100% Accurate:** It doesn't guess. If the word follows the rules
    programmed into the FST, it will be parsed flawlessly every single time.
2.  **Bi-directional:** An FST can work in reverse (generation). It can take a
    root word and a tag (e.g., **walk** + **Past Tense**) and mathematically
    generate the correct output (**walked**).
3.  **Lightweight:** It doesn't require massive supercomputers to run or train.

## Beyond AI: Dictionaries, Lexicons, and Glossing

While improving AI is a massive benefit, computational grammar and morphological
parsing are equally vital for traditional linguistic tools. For a highly
fusional and mutating language like Irish, building a functional dictionary or
digital lexicon is not as simple as alphabetizing a list of words.

### The Dictionary Problem: Finding the Root

If you encounter the word _"*mbád*"_ (their boat) or "_bhfear_" (of a man) in an
Irish text, you cannot simply look up "m" or "b" in a standard dictionary.
Because of rules like **eclipsis** (\*_urú_) and **lenition** (_séimhiú_), the
first letter of an Irish word frequently changes based on its grammatical
context.

A traditional dictionary requires the reader to already know that the root of
_"*mbád*"_ is _bád_ (boat), and the root of _"*bhfear*"_ is _fear_ (man). For a
learner—or a basic search engine—sorting words by their first letter completely
breaks down in Irish.

A morphological parser solves this root-finding problem automatically. By
formally reversing the mutation and inflection rules, the parser mathematically
strips away the grammatical "wrapping" and reliably returns the definitive,
dictionary-form stem (lemma) of any given word.

### Grammatical Analysis and Text Glossing

For linguists, historians, and students analyzing Irish texts, **glossing**—the
practice of providing a word-by-word grammatical breakdown of sentences—is
incredibly tedious to perform manually.

A formal computational grammar automates this. It can rapidly read a complex,
inflected text and output a precise grammatical tag for every single word. This
ensures that researchers can search databases of Irish literature for specific
grammatical constructions (e.g., "Find all examples of the past habitual tense")
rather than just searching for fixed spellings, unlocking far deeper analytical
capabilities for the study and preservation of the language.
