# Literature Review: Finite State Transducer (FST) Solutions for Irish Morphology

The intersection of Finite State Transducers (FSTs) and Irish language
morphology is a specialized but well-established area of Computational
Linguistics. Because Irish (a Goidelic Celtic language) features complex
concatenative morphology, initial consonant mutations (lenition and eclipsis),
and significant allomorphy, FSTs have been the technology of choice for
morphological analysis and generation.

Below is an extensive scan of the notable publications, researchers, and
institutions leading the development of FST-based solutions for the Irish
language.

---

## 1. Key Researchers & Innovators

### Dr. Elaine Uí Dhonnchadha

**Institution:** Trinity College Dublin (TCD), Centre for Language and
Communication Studies. **Focus:** Uí Dhonnchadha is arguably the most prominent
figure in the specific niche of FST-based Irish morphological analysis. She has
extensively researched and built robust inflectional morphology engines capable
of handling unrestricted Irish text. **Contributions:**

- She designed a comprehensive Part-of-Speech (POS) tagger and morphological
  analyzer for Irish that combines finite-state morphology with Constraint
  Grammar (CG) disambiguation rules.
- Her systems have successfully modeled the morphotactics of Irish verbs, nouns,
  and adjectives, achieving high accuracy (approx. 95% tagging accuracy and 93%+
  token coverage on corpora).
- Her work forms the foundation for many modern syntactically annotated corpora
  for Irish (such as the Irish Dependency Treebank).

### Prof. Kevin Scannell

**Institution:** Saint Louis University. **Focus:** Professor Scannell is
globally renowned for his work on natural language processing for
lesser-resourced languages, with a particular emphasis on Irish.
**Contributions:**

- He is the creator of several widely used Irish language tools, including _An
  Gramadóir_ (grammar checker) and _An Caighdeánaitheoir_ (The Standardiser).
- _An Caighdeánaitheoir_ internally relies on an FST backbone to recognize
  historical, dialectal, and non-standard Irish word forms and transduce them
  into the official modern standard (_An Caighdeán Oifigiúil_).
- His work demonstrates the practical, user-facing applications of FSTs in
  spelling and grammar correction software.

### Other Notable Contributors

- **Prof. Josef van Genabith**: Often a co-author with Elaine Uí Dhonnchadha on
  papers detailing the design and testing of Irish NLP tools.
- **Various researchers** focusing on historical linguistics have utilized tools
  like `foma` to build FST models for **Old and Middle Irish**, mapping complex
  historical verb stems and sound changes.

## 2. Key Institutions and Research Centers

1.  **Trinity College Dublin (TCD) - Centre for Language and Communication
    Studies / ADAPT Centre**: The primary hub for the development of Irish
    computational morphology, treebanks, and POS taggers using FSTs.
2.  **Dublin City University (DCU)**: DCU has a strong NLP group and is involved
    in Irish MT (Machine Translation) projects where morphological analysis is a
    crucial pre-processing step.
3.  **Saint Louis University**: Base of operations for Kevin Scannell's
    extensive open-source Irish NLP resources.
4.  **Foras na Gaeilge & Gaois (Fiontar & Scoil na Gaeilge, DCU)**: While
    primarily focused on lexicography and terminology, their massive dataset
    projects frequently require the kind of morphological normalization that
    FSTs provide.

## 4. Methodologies Utilized

The literature consistently highlights a few standard approaches to building
these parsers:

1.  **Xerox Finite-State Tools (XFST) & LEXC:** Many of the foundational Irish
    analyzers were written using XFST to define the replace-rules (for mutations
    like eclipsis and lenition) and LEXC to define the lexicons and
    morphotactics (stem + suffix combinations).
2.  **Foma:** The open-source alternative to XFST, heavily utilized in more
    modern or open-source adaptations of Irish morphology rules.
3.  **Constraint Grammar (vislcg3):** FSTs in Irish are almost always paired
    with Constraint Grammar. Because Irish morphology generates high ambiguity
    (e.g., _a_ can mean "his", "her", "their", or act as a vocative particle,
    numerical particle, or relative pronoun), the FST outputs all possible
    analyses, and the CG rules use sentence context to filter out the incorrect
    ones.

## Conclusion

The literature reveals that FSTs are an absolute necessity for robust Irish NLP.
The transition from legacy tools (like XFST) to modern Python-integrated
libraries like **Pynini** represents the next logical step in this research
lineage, promising to make the extensive linguistic groundwork laid by
researchers like Uí Dhonnchadha and Scannell compatible with the modern era of
deep learning and neural NLP pipelines.
