# Morphology - An Overview

To build a computational parser for any language, one must first understand the
architectural blueprints of how that language constructs meaning. Modern Irish
(<span class="ga-word">Gaeilge<span class="ga-tooltip">/ɟɛːlʲɟɛ/</span></span>) is a Goidelic Celtic language that presents fascinating challenges
and unique structural paradigms compared to widespread Indo-European languages
like English or Spanish.

This document serves as high-level overview of the core morphological and
syntactic aspects of Irish, and outlines our strategy for modeling these
components using Pynini-based Finite State Transducers (FSTs).

## 1. High-Level Typology and Syntax

### The VSO Word Order

The most immediately striking feature of Irish syntax is its **VSO
(Verb-Subject-Object)** phrase structure. Unlike the SVO (Subject-Verb-Object)
structure of English, every standard declarative sentence in Irish begins with
the verb.

For example, the English sentence *"The man saw the boat"* translates to:

> <span class="ga-word">Chonaic an fear an bád<span class="ga-tooltip">/xɔnˠəc anˠ fʲaɾʲ anˠ bˠaːd̪ˠ/</span></span> (Saw the man the boat).

This rigid initial placement gives the Verb Phrase (VP) primacy in the sentence
structure. It anchors the clause, establishing tense, mood, and polarity
(positive/negative/interrogative) right at the outset.

### The Verb Phrase: The Engine of the Sentence

Because the verb comes first, it often fuses with particles that determine the
nature of the sentence. A verbal particle preceding the verb dictates whether
the statement is a question (<span class="ga-word">An<span class="ga-tooltip">/anˠ/</span></span>), a negative statement (<span class="ga-word">Ní<span class="ga-tooltip">/nʲɪː/</span></span>), or a past-tense
marker (<span class="ga-word">Do<span class="ga-tooltip">/d̪ˠɔ/</span></span>).

These preceding particles trigger **initial consonant mutations** on the
following verb stem. For instance, the root verb <span class="ga-word">bris<span class="ga-tooltip">/bʲɾʲɪʃ/</span></span> (break):

- Positive Past: <span class="ga-word">Bhris sé<span class="ga-tooltip">/vʲɾʲɪʃ ʃɛː/</span></span> (He broke) - Lenition triggered.
- Negative Past: <span class="ga-word">Níor bhris sé<span class="ga-tooltip">/nʲɪːɔɾˠ vʲɾʲɪʃ ʃɛː/</span></span> (He didn't break) - Lenition triggered.
- Dependent Interrogative: <span class="ga-word">An mbriseann tú?<span class="ga-tooltip">/anˠ mʲɾʲɪʃəːn̠ʲ t̪ˠʊː/</span></span> (Do you break?) - Eclipsis
  triggered.

Furthermore, Irish verbs can be **analytic** (requiring a separate subject
pronoun, like <span class="ga-word">briseann sé<span class="ga-tooltip">/bʲɾʲɪʃəːn̠ʲ ʃɛː/</span></span> "he breaks") or **synthetic** (where the subject
pronoun is baked directly into the verbal suffix, like <span class="ga-word">brisim<span class="ga-tooltip">/bʲɾʲɪʃəmʲ/</span></span> "I break").

### Noun Phrases and Declensions

Nouns in Irish are categorized by two genders (masculine and feminine) and are
heavily inflected across four cases: Nominative/Accusative (used as the base
form), Genitive (showing possession), Vocative (direct address), and Dative
(following prepositions).

Similar to verbs, Noun Phrases (NPs) are subject to complex initial consonant
mutations depending on preceding words (articles, possessive adjectives,
prepositions, or numbers).

- <span class="ga-word">Mála<span class="ga-tooltip">/mˠaːlˠa/</span></span> (A bag) → <span class="ga-word">Mo mhála<span class="ga-tooltip">/mˠɔ waːlˠa/</span></span> (My bag) → <span class="ga-word">Ár mBála<span class="ga-tooltip">/aːɾˠ mˠaːlˠa/</span></span> (Our bag)

Additionally, Irish utilizes a complex system of **five noun declensions**,
determining how a noun alters its suffix to form plurals or genitive cases
(often involving "slenderizing" or "broadening" the final consonant, a process
called palatalization).

## 2. Staging the Pynini FST Implementation

Modeling this intricate web of interdependent rules requires a systematic,
bottom-up approach. We will utilize Pynini (a Python OpenFst wrapper) to build
our computational morphology engine in stages. FSTs are perfectly suited for
this because they can cleanly model **concatenative morphology** (adding
prefixes/suffixes) and apply **replace rules** (for mutations) simultaneously.

We will stage the development of our FSTs in the following order:

### Stage 1: The Morphophonemic Foundation (Mutations & Palatalization)

Before we can parse words, we must teach the engine how Irish sounds change.

- **Goal:** Build "dumb" transducer rules that simply know <span class="ga-word">how<span class="ga-tooltip">/hˠɔw/</span></span> to apply
  phonological changes, completely independent of grammar.
- **Implementation:** We will create Pynini `cdrewrite` replace-rules for
  **Lenition** (e.g., mapping `b` to `bh`), **Eclipsis** (e.g., mapping `b` to
  `mb`), and **Palatalization/Depalatalization** (slenderizing broad consonants
  and vice-versa).
- **Reasoning:** These are the atomic building blocks of Irish morphology. Every
  subsequent noun, verb, or adjective paradigm will need to call back to these
  core phonological rules.

### Stage 2: The Nominal Domain (Nouns and Adjectives)

Once the FST can mutate consonants, we move to the vocabulary.

- **Goal:** Model the noun phrase and adjective agreement.
- **Implementation:** We will build a lexicon transducer (using Pynini`s string
  compilation maps) defining root lemmas grouped by their declension class (1
  through 5). We will then build suffix transducers that concatenate the correct
  case/number endings to the roots based on their declension, and apply
  palatalization rules from Stage 1 where necessary.
- **Reasoning:** Nouns are easily categorized into the five declensions,
  providing a highly structured and contained dataset to test the concatenative
  abilities of our FST design before tackling the more fluid Verb phrases.

### Stage 3: The Verbal Domain

The most complex part of Irish morphology due to synthetic/analytic forms,
tense, mood, and dependent/independent forms.

- **Goal:** Construct the verbal paradigms across both major conjugations.
- **Implementation:** We will define verb roots and append tense/person markers.
  Crucially, we will map verbal particles (like <span class="ga-word">An<span class="ga-tooltip">/anˠ/</span></span>, <span class="ga-word">Ní<span class="ga-tooltip">/nʲɪː/</span></span>, <span class="ga-word">Do<span class="ga-tooltip">/d̪ˠɔ/</span></span>, <span class="ga-word">Gur<span class="ga-tooltip">/ɡʊɾˠ/</span></span>) to the
  mutation rules established in Stage 1, ensuring the verbal root mutates
  correctly based on the preceding particle.
- **Reasoning:** Verbs require the engine to make decisions based on preceding
  context (particles). By waiting until Stage 3, we ensure our mutation rewrite
  rules are bug-free and robust.

### Stage 4: Integration and Morphotactics

The final stage brings the components together.

- **Goal:** Combine the Noun, Adjective, and Verb transducers into a single
  unified morphological analyzer/generator.
- **Implementation:** We take the union of all our sub-transducers. We then wrap
  this in a Python API that can accept a raw string of Irish text, tokenize it,
  and pass it through the unified FST to return all statistically viable
  morphological tags (e.g., parsing "mbáid" →
  `bád + Noun + Masc + Plural + Nom + Eclipsed`).
- **Reasoning:** This creates the actual production-ready tool that is prepared
  to be handed off to the neural (LLM) components of the neuro-symbolic pipeline
  for semantic disambiguation.

### Stage 5: Phonotactics and Connected Speech

The final phonetic refinement stage.

- **Goal:** Model the "connected speech" effects that bridge the gap between word-level IPA and natural language.
- **Implementation:** We will implement **Sandhi rules** (like the elision of 'ag' before consonants) and **vowel reduction** in proclitics. These are added as a post-processing transducer layer that acts on the output of the unified morphological FST.
- **Reasoning:** Modeling isolated words is sufficient for dictionary lookup, but for natural language processing or TTS applications, the engine must handle the firm rules of how words influence each other's pronunciation across boundaries.

### Stage 6: Neural Generation and Translation via LLM

Finally, we close the loop with the neural component.

- **Goal:** Produce accurate and contextually appropriate surface text in Irish
  from semantic intent using massive parallel reasoning capabilities.
- **Implementation:** The LLM receives the source text (e.g., English) and
  determines the abstract linguistic structures needed for translation. It uses
  its internal semantic understanding to generate a string of abstract
  morphological tags (e.g., `[bád + Noun + Masc + Plural + Nom + Eclipsed]`).
  This abstract output is then passed back down to the unified Pynini FST from
  the previous stages, which acts as a deterministic guardrail and transduces it into the
  flawless surface text (`mbáid`).
- **Reasoning:** LLMs excel at understanding context, semantic intent, and
  translation logic, but frequently fail at memorizing strict cellular rules and
  paradigms of under-resourced complex languages. By staging the pipeline this
  way, the LLM makes the high-level decisions, and the FST enforces the grammar
  perfectly.
