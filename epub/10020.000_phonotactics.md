# Phonotactics: <br><span style="font-size: 0.5em;">cleaning to make the final surface form of the word</span>

The **Phonotactics** layer is the last stage in the morphotactics conversion. It takes the output of the morphology layer and assembles the final surface form of the word. In the case of the Irish language this involves removing any epenthetic vowels that were added during the morphology stage and ensuring that the final surface form of the word is a valid Irish word or forcing '<ga ipa="/kɪːlˠ lʲɛ kɪːlˠ aɡəsˠ lʲahənˠ lʲɛ lʲahənˠ/">Caol le Caol agus Leathan le Leathan</ga>' to ensure the correct context of consonants.

This is a classic conflict in computational linguistics: **Lexical Phonology** (the engine) vs. **Post-Lexical/Prosodic Phonology** (the broad transcription).

Using the first level FSTs, we are generating the "Canonical Form." However, for a natural language processing (NLP) task or a TTS (Text-to-Speech) application, the "Sentence Level" (connected speech) is almost always superior.

Here is an analysis of why the two outputs differ and why the merged version is generally the "correct" target for Irish.

---

### 1. The "Sandhi" Effect: `ag` $→$ `/ə/`
In Irish, the prepositional particle *ag* (used for the progressive aspect) is a **proclitic**. It rarely carries lexical stress.
* **Your Engine:** Treates <ga ipa="/aɡ/">ag</ga> as an isolated lexical unit. It retains the voiced velar plosive `/ɡ/`.
* **Google/Sentence Level:** Recognizes that in connected speech, the final consonant of *ag* is elided before another consonant.
    * *Rule:* `ag` + [Consonant] $→$ `/ə/`
    * *Rule:* `ag` + [Vowel] $→$ `/əɡ/` (e.g., <ga ipa="/aɡ ɔːlˠ/">ag ól</ga> $→$ `/əɡ oːlˠ/`)



### 2. Stress and Vowel Reduction
Your engine marks every word with primary stress (`ˈ`). In a longish sentence like the one provided, this is prosodically impossible for a native speaker. 
* **Sentence Level:** Functional words (articles like *na*, particles like *ag*, prepositions like *do*) undergo **vowel reduction** to a schwa `/ə/` because they are unstressed.
* **Word Level:** Preserves the full vowel quality (e.g., your `/nˠa/` vs. the sentence `/nə/`).

### 3. Dialectal vs. Formal Accuracy
Your engine’s output contains some phonetic choices that look more like a "spelling-to-sound" mapping than a phonological model:
* **The Verb:** You have `/ˈvʲɪːɔɣ/`. Note that in most modern dialects, the final `-dh` in *bhíodh* is pronounced as `/x/` (Broad) or even `/v/` in the South, but rarely as the voiced `/ɣ/` in this specific grammatical position.
* **The Cluster:** In *shéasúr*, your engine produced `/ˈhɛːasˠəːɾˠ/`. However, the **sh-** mutation in Irish results in a simple `/h/` sound. Your engine seems to be trying to pronounce the "s" and the "h" separately or is over-specifying the vowel hiatus.

### My Opinion: Which approach should you use?

Given your background in **Linguistics PhD research** and **XBRL standards**, the answer depends on your **application**:

#### Use Word-Level (Your Engine) IF:
* You are building a **Morphological Analyzer** or an FST where the goal is to map surface strings to abstract lemmas.

#### Use Sentence-Level (Google/Connected Speech) IF:
* **The Goal is Realism:** If you want to model how the language is actually spoken.

* **FST Refinement:** You can actually add a **Sandhi Level** to your FST. After your lexical and morphological tapes, you add a "Phonological Phrase" tape that applies rules like:
