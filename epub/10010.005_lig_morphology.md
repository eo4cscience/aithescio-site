# Morphology and the Leipzig Interlinear Gloss

The Leipzig Interlinear Gloss (LIG) provides a structured, four-line representation of linguistic data to bridge the gap between source text and grammatical analysis. By aligning morphemes with their specific functions, LIG allows linguists and computational engineers to visualize the exact relationship between surface forms and underlying morphological roles. 

This format is particularly useful for languages with complex morphophonology like Irish, where initial mutations and synthetic verb forms can obscure the underlying structure.

## Sample Analysis: Complex Irish Sentence

**Irish Sentence:**
> Bhíodh na hiascairí ag cur na mbád in oiriúint do shéasúr an earraigh.

**Morphological Functions**

| Gloss | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **bí.PAST-HABIT.3SG.AUT** | Past Habitual/Imperfect |
| **na.DEF.ART.PL** | Definite Article Plural |
| **HIATUS-iasc-ER.PL** | H-prothetic + Noun + Agentive suffix + Plural |
| **ag.PROG** | Progressive particle |
| **cuir.VN** | Verbal Noun |
| **na.DEF.ART.GEN.PL** | Genitive Plural Article |
| **ECL-bád.GEN.PL** | Eclipsed Genitive Plural Noun |
| **i.prep** | Preposition |
| **oiriúint.VN** | Verbal Noun / Dative |
| **do.prep** | Preposition |
| **LEN-séasúr** | Lenited Noun |
| **an.DEF.ART.GEN.SG** | Genitive Singular Article |
| **earrach.GEN.SG** | Genitive Singular Noun |

**English Translation:**
> The fishermen used to be preparing the boats for the spring season.

## Linguistic Notes for the FST Mindset

- **Initial Mutations:** Notice the **m-** in *mbád* (eclipsis triggered by the genitive plural article) and the **sh-** in *shéasúr* (lenition triggered by the preposition *do*). In an FST model, these would typically be handled as two-level rules where the trigger (the preceding article or preposition) dictates the surface form of the initial consonant.
- **Prothesis:** The **h-** in *hiascairí* prevents hiatus after the vowel-ending article *na*.
- **The Genitive Case:** Irish morphology relies heavily on internal vowel changes (broad vs. slender) for case marking, seen here in *earrach* (nominative) vs. *earraigh* (genitive).

- **Inflectional Paradigms & Vowel Quality:** To determine if a suffix is broad or slender in Irish, one must look at the inflectional paradigm rather than just the individual letters. In the case of the *Past Habitual*, the suffix is inherently broad, which dictates the orthography; the "o" in *bhíodh* acts as a quality marker or "buffer," shielding the broad dental consonant from the preceding slender vowel. This quality is determined by the morphological category—where the Past Habitual requires a broad ending while the Past Participle might be slender—and the insertion of these vowel signals ensures that the final consonant maintains its intended phonological value regardless of the root's internal phonology.
