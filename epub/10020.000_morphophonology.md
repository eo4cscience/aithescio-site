The **Morphophonology** layer is the first stage in the morpho-phonology conversion. It takes an semantic intent and assembles the first level of the words involved.

As we now from the previous pages the lexicon provides the stems and the properties of the words. The morphophonology layer takes these stems and properties and assembles them into words.

## Understanding the Terms

To build a successful FST, we must first distinguish between these two
linguistic concepts and how they manifest in Irish:

### 1. Morphophonology

**Morphophonology** is the study of how the sound (or spelling) of a morpheme
changes based on its grammatical context. In many languages, these changes occur
at the end of a word (suffixation), but in Irish, the most famous
morphophonological changes occur at the beginning\*.

- **Example (Initial Mutations):** The root word cat (cat) becomes <ga ipa="/mň†…Ē xatŐ™ň†/">mo chat</ga> (my
  cat) through **lenition** (<ga ipa="/ É…õňźv ≤…™ äňź/">s√©imhi√ļ</ga>) or <ga ipa="/aňź…ĺň† …°atŐ™ň†/">√°r gcat</ga> (our cat) through
  **eclipsis** (<ga ipa="/ ä…ĺň†…ôňź/">ur√ļ</ga>). The 'c' changes to 'ch' or 'gc' purely because of the
  preceding possessive pronoun.

### 2. Phonotactics

**Phonotactics** refers to the rules that govern the permissible combinations of
sounds (and by extension, letters) in a language. It dictates the "legal" shape
of a word's spelling and phonology, regardless of its grammatical meaning.

- **Example (Vowel Harmony):** The foundational Irish rule of **<ga ipa="/k…™ňźlň† l ≤…õ k…™ňźlň† a…°…ôsň† l ≤ah…ônň† l ≤…õ l ≤ah…ônň†/">Caol le caol
  agus leathan le leathan</ga> ** ("Slender with slender and broad with broad") is a
  phonotactic constraint. It mandates that if a consonant is "slender"
  (palatalized), it must be surrounded by slender vowels (i, e). If it is
  "broad" (velarized), it must be surrounded by broad vowels (a, o, u).

## eGrammar Coverage: The Core Requirements

For the eGrammar to accurately model Irish, this layer must comprehensively handle
several distinct phenomena:

### I. Initial Consonant Mutations

The FST must be able to recognize and generate the two primary mutation types
triggered by particles, articles, and possessives:

- **Lenition (<ga ipa="/ É…õňźv ≤…™ äňź/">S√©imhi√ļ</ga>):** Inserting an 'h' after certain consonants (e.g.,
  `b ‚Üí bh`, `p ‚Üí ph`).
- **Eclipsis (<ga ipa="/ ä…ĺň†…ôňź/">Ur√ļ</ga>):** Prepending a voicing or nasalizing consonant (e.g.,
  `b ‚Üí mb`, `p ‚Üí bp`, `c ‚Üí gc`).
- **T-prothesis & H-prothesis:** Handling special markers added to vowel-initial
  words (e.g., `ar√°n ‚Üí an t-ar√°n`, `uisce ‚Üí na huisce`).

### II. Orthographic Harmony (Vowel Quality)

When the **Morphotactic Layer** adds a suffix (like a plural ending), the
**Morphophonology Layer** must ensure the spelling satisfies the "Slender with
Slender" rule.

- **Example:** If we add the plural suffix `-anna` to the slender-ending root
  <ga ipa="/b ≤…õh/">beith</ga> (birch), the FST must realize that `-anna` (broad) cannot follow `th`
  (slender). It must "harmonize" the suffix, often by inserting a silent
  epenthetic vowel creating *<ga ipa="/b ≤…õhaňźnŐ† ≤…ô/">beitheanna</ga>*.

### III. Syncopation

Irish frequently drops vowels from the internal syllable of a word when a suffix
is added. This prevents the word from becoming too "long" or phonetically
unstable.

- **Example:** The verb <ga ipa="/…™m ≤…ô…ĺ ≤/">imir</ga> (play) becomes <ga ipa="/…™m ≤…ĺ ≤…ôňźm ≤/">imr√≠m</ga> (I play), not <ga ipa="/…™m ≤…ô…ĺ ≤…™ňźm ≤/">imir√≠m</ga>.
  The middle 'i' is syncopated (deleted) during the inflection process. The FST
  must explicitly model these deletion rules to avoid generating non-existent
  forms.

### IV. Epenthesis

In certain consonant clusters, especially in dialects, a "helping" or epenthetic
vowel is pronounced (and sometimes written) to make the word easier to say.
While less frequent in modern standard orthography, an advanced FST must
recognize these shifts to handle dialectal variations.

### V. Dental Sandhi (The DNTLS Rule)

One of the more subtle rules in Irish phonotactics is the blocking of lenition
when two dental consonants (*d, n, t, l, s*) meet at a boundary. Despite a
grammatical rule demanding lenition, if the preceding word ends in a dental and
the following word begins with one, the mutation is often suppressed.

- **Example:** The definite article *an* ends in 'n' (dental). The feminine noun
  *teanga* (language) should normally lenite after the article (*an theanga*),
  but because 't' is also a dental, the mutation is blocked: *an teanga*.

### VI. Provection (De-voicing)

Provection occurs when a voiced consonant becomes voiceless, typically when it
meets a voiceless suffix like the past participle marker *-tha* or *-te*. This
is a crucial morphophonological shift that the FST must resolve to generate
correct surface spellings.

- **Example:** The verb root *<ga ipa="/ Éc…ĺ ≤…™ňź…Ēw/">scr√≠obh</ga>* (write) ends in a voiced 'bh'. When the
  past participle suffix is added, the 'bh' and 'th' fuse into a voiceless 'f':
  *scr√≠ofa*.

### VII. Consonant Quality Shifts (Palatalization)

While vowel harmony (*Caol le Caol*) often dictates spelling, the underlying
consonant quality (broad vs. slender) can shift independently as part of the
morphological process. 

- **Example:** In the transition from the nominative *b√°d* (boat - broad 'd') to
  the genitive singular *b√°id* (of a boat - slender 'd'), the consonant itself
  changes quality. The FST must model these "deep" quality shifts which then
  trigger the secondary orthographic harmony of the surrounding vowels.



## The Morphophonological Pipeline: Order of Composition

From an implementation perspective, these morphophonological rules cannot be applied in an arbitrary order. Because many of these rules are dependencies of one another‚ÄĒwhere the output of one shift triggers the necessity of another‚ÄĒwe must sequence the FST composition carefully to ensure linguistically valid results.

The optimal pipeline starts with **Deep Quality Adjustment**, where the underlying consonant quality (palatalization or de-palatalization) is first established. It is important to note that the Morphophonology layer does not "know" about grammatical cases like the Genitive; instead, it responds to **abstract trigger tags** (e.g., `^PAL`) provided by the preceding Morphotactic layer. This fundamental shift is the primary trigger for all subsequent spelling and phonetic changes. Following this, **Structural Rules** such as syncopation and epenthesis are applied to modify the syllable skeleton of the word, deleting or adding vowels as the specific instructional markers require.

Only after the word's skeleton is stable do we apply **Boundary Transformations**, including both internal provection (consonant de-voicing) and external initial mutations. These rules resolve how the word's edges interact with instructions for markers or preceding particles. The final active layer in the word-level pipeline is **Phonotactic Realization**, which performs a global pass of vowel harmony (*Caol le Caol*) to ensure that all previous shifts have resulted in a string that conforms to Irish orthographic laws. The very last stage, often occurring during sentence-level composition, is **Syntactic Sandhi (DNTLS)**, which acts as a final conditional filter to block mutations when specific phonetic constraints are met across word boundaries.

This sequence‚ÄĒ`Deep Quality ‚ąė Structural Shifts ‚ąė Boundary Transformations ‚ąė Orthographic Realization ‚ąė Sandhi`‚ÄĒprovides a robust, mathematically sound framework for modeling the full complexity of the Irish language while maintaining a clean separation of concerns between grammar (why a change happens) and phonology (how a change happens).
